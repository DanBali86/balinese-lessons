01

Sentence Structure

S   Subject
+
V   Verb
+
O   Object (optional)
+
Time word
Key rule: Balinese follows the same Subject-Verb-Object order as English. This makes building sentences intuitive from the start.
Time words like mani (tomorrow) and ibi (yesterday) go at the end of the sentence — not the beginning like in English. Your tutor showed this clearly with the sentence example at the bottom of the first page.
No verb conjugation for tense. The verb doesn't change form. Time is expressed entirely by adding a time word. "Tiang ngajeng" means "I eat" — add "ibi" and it becomes "I ate yesterday." Add "mani" and it becomes "I will eat tomorrow."

Negation
To make any sentence negative, insert ten directly after the subject, before the verb. Your tutor wrote this in red — it is inserted cleanly: Tiang → Tiang ten → Tiang ten ngajeng. "Ten" is the everyday (Madia/Kasar) negative. The formal (Alus) equivalent is nenten.

Question Words from your notes

Question type Balinese word Position in sentence Example
Yes/No Y/N ? Rising intonation only — no extra word needed Ragane ngajeng ibi?
Wh- (general) Wh ? Front of sentence Dije ragane ngajeng ibi?
Where Dije Sentence-initial Dije ragane ngajeng ibi? — Where did you eat yesterday?
Pattern to learn
Your tutor circled Dije for emphasis. For yes/no questions, no question word is added — intonation (rising pitch at the end) does the work. For "where" questions, Dije fronts the whole sentence, and the rest stays in S-V-O-Time order unchanged.

Dan vs Lan
Your notes show dan — lan for "and." Both are used — lan is more distinctly Balinese, while dan is shared with Indonesian. Your tutor likely taught both as they're interchangeable in everyday speech. When in doubt, use lan.
07

Practice Drills

Based on this lesson — drill these until automatic
  • Say the sentence pattern out loud: Tiang ngajeng ibi. Tiang ten ngajeng ibi. Tiang ngajeng mani. Notice the verb never changes — only the time word and the negation slot shift.
  • Swap the subject through all pronouns: Tiang ngajeng. Ragane ngajeng. Ia ngajeng. Ia mekejang ngajeng. Keep the verb constant.
  • Build the ordering sentence from memory: Tiang lakar pesan nasi campur besik. Then change the number: dua, telu, pat.
  • Practice the where-question: Dije Ragane ngajeng ibi? Then answer it: Tiang ngajeng di jumah ibi. (I ate at home yesterday.)
  • Negate three sentences: take any positive sentence, insert ten after the subject. Drill until the word order feels natural.
  • Swap verbs: Tiang minum ibi. Tiang nyakan mani. Ragane pesan nasi campur?
