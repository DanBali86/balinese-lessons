Lesson 09

Adjectives & Descriptors

Overview
In Balinese, adjectives come after the noun — the opposite of English. So "a big house" is umah gede (house big). This rule is consistent and once you have it, you can describe almost anything. This lesson builds your core descriptor vocabulary and locks in the noun-adjective order.
Word Order Rule

Noun + Adjective (not Adjective + Noun)

English: big house  →  Balinese: umah gede (house big)

English: cold water  →  Balinese: yeh nyem (water cold)

English: delicious food  →  Balinese: ajengan enak (food delicious)

Umah gede nika u-mah · ge-de · ni-ka That house is big
Yeh nyem pesan yeh · nyem · pe-san The water is very cold

Practice
  • Build five noun-adjective phrases using words you already know: umah gede, yeh nyem, ajengan melah, kopi panas, umah cenik.
  • Add the intensifier pesan: umah gede pesan (very big house), kopi panas pesan (very hot coffee).
  • Make descriptive sentences: Tiang dot yeh nyem (I want cold water). Umah nika gede pesan (That house is very big).
  • Notice: adjectives don't change form for gender or number. Gede works for one or many, male or female.

Question Words

Overview
You already know dije (where) from your tutor session. This lesson completes your full question-word toolkit. In Balinese, most question words go at the front of the sentence, and the rest of the sentence keeps its normal S-V-O order. Mastering these unlocks real two-way conversation.

Dijedi-jeWhereSentence-initial
Napi / Apena-pi / a-peWhatNapi = Alus · Ape = Kasar
Sirasi-raWhoAlus; everyday = nyen
Kenkenken-kenHow / what kindAlso used for "how are you"
Pidanpi-danWhen
Ngudangu-daWhyDistinctly Balinese
Kudaku-daHow many / how muchSeen in "aji kuda" (how much does it cost)
Question Sentence Examples
Napi ragane ngajeng?
What are you eating?
Sira teka ibi?
Who came yesterday?
Pidan ragane luas?
When are you leaving?
Nguda ragane ten ngajeng?
Why didn't you eat?
Kenken ragane?
How are you? / How is it going?
Practice
  • Drill each question word with a simple sentence. Start with Napi ragane nginum? (What are you drinking?) and swap the question word through the full list.
  • Combine with time words: Pidan ragane teka mani? (When are you coming tomorrow?) — notice both a question word and a time word in one sentence.
  • Practice answering your own questions to make it a full exchange.

Lesson 11

Common Verbs

Overview
You know ngajeng, minum, and nyakan from your tutor session. This lesson adds 20 essential verbs that cover most of daily life. Remember: Balinese verbs do not conjugate for tense, person, or number. The same verb works for I, you, he, she, we, they, past, present, and future. Time words and context carry the meaning.

Insight
No Conjugation — Ever
Tiang luas. (I go / I went / I will go.) Ia luas. (He/she goes.) Ia mekejang luas. (They all go.) The verb luas never changes. Add a time word to specify tense: Tiang luas mani (I'm going tomorrow). Tiang luas ibi (I went yesterday). This is one of the features that makes Balinese more logical than English once you get used to it.
Practice
  • Pick five verbs and build a sentence for each using a different pronoun and time word every time.
  • Make a negative: Tiang ten nawang (I don't know). Ia ten teka ibi (She didn't come yesterday).
  • Combine a question word with a new verb: Dije ragane meli? (Where did you buy it?) Pidan ia teka? (When does he arrive?)

Lesson 12

Directions & Navigation

Overview
Balinese uses a completely different directional system from English — and from most languages. Instead of north/south/east/west or left/right, traditional Balinese orientation is based on Kaja (toward the mountain) and Kelod (toward the sea), with Kangin (sunrise/east) and Kauh (sunset/west) as the cross-axis. These directions are sacred and used in everyday speech, temple orientation, and even how you position yourself when sleeping. You will hear them constantly.

Culture Note
Kaja-Kelod as Spiritual Axis The mountain-sea axis is not just geographical — it is spiritually loaded. Kaja (mountain, toward Gunung Agung) is the direction of the gods and purity. Kelod (sea) is associated with the lower, earthly realm. Temples always face kaja. When sleeping, Balinese traditionally place their head kaja. When a Balinese person says "go a bit kelod," they are giving you a real directional instruction that reflects how they experience the landscape. Learning kaja and kelod is not optional if you want to communicate naturally.
Practice
  • Figure out which direction is kaja from where you live in Denpasar — it should be roughly north toward Gunung Agung. Notice how locals use it in conversation.
  • Practice giving directions combining kaja/kelod/kangin/kauh with luas: Luas kelod, buin luas kauh.
  • Drill the three location words: dini (here), ditu (there), dija (over there). Use them in sentences with di: Tiang di dini (I am here).

Emotions & States

Overview
Expressing how you feel in Balinese opens up genuine human connection. The structure is simple: Tiang + [emotion/state]. Many emotional expressions in Balinese are tied to the heart or liver (hati), which is considered the emotional center — just as in many other Asian languages.

Culture Note
Keneh — More Than "Feeling" The word keneh is one of the most culturally loaded words in Balinese. It means mind, heart, feeling, intention, and disposition all at once. When someone asks kenken kenehe? they are asking about your whole inner state, not just your mood. Balinese culture places enormous weight on maintaining inner balance — being keneh melah (a good/clear mind-heart) is a life goal, not just a passing state. Using this word correctly will mark you as someone who genuinely understands Balinese culture.

Colours & Descriptions

Overview
Colours in Balinese carry ritual significance beyond everyday use. Each of the four cardinal directions has a sacred colour associated with it in the Balinese Hindu tradition, and these appear in temple offerings, ceremonial dress, and spiritual symbolism. Learn the colours as both everyday descriptors and cultural markers.

Napi warna selegang ragane?

Culture Note
The Colours of the Compass At Balinese temples and in ceremonial offerings you will see cloths, flowers, and decorations arranged in the four directional colours — white, red, yellow, black — each placed facing its sacred direction. The centre point is represented by poleng, the black-and-white checked cloth you see everywhere in Bali, which represents the balance of opposites. You will notice poleng wrapped around trees, statues, and temple gates. It is not decoration — it is protection and balance.

At the Market

Overview
The pasar (market) is one of the best places to practice real Balinese. Vendors are accustomed to quick exchanges and will appreciate any effort. This lesson combines everything from Phase 1 and Phase 2 — numbers, adjectives, verbs, and question words — into real market conversation.

Practice
  • Go to a local market and try at least one exchange in Balinese. Even just Aji kuda niki? is a win.
  • Practice the full negotiation sequence from memory: greeting → ask price → say it's too expensive → ask for a discount → agree on a number → buy.
  • Add adjectives: Tiang dot meli buah melah (I want to buy good fruit). Buah nika nyen apa tara? (Is that fruit fresh?)

Weather & Nature

Overview
Weather is one of the most natural conversation openers in any language. Bali has two seasons — dry (sasih kapat) and wet (sasih kasa) — and the landscape vocabulary is deeply tied to the island's geography. Nature words also connect to ceremony and spiritual life.

The Home

Overview
A Balinese home (umah) is not just a house — it is a sacred compound organized around a family temple (sanggah) and governed by the same kaja-kelod-kangin-kauh axis as the wider world. Understanding the parts of a Balinese home helps you navigate conversations about daily life and gives you insight into how Balinese people organize their world.
Parts of the Home
BalinesePronunciationEnglishNote
Umahu-mahHouse / homeThe whole compound
Sanggahsang-gahFamily templeAlways in kaja-kangin corner
Pa onpa-onKitchenFrom your tutor session
Baleba-lePavilion / open platformMulti-purpose gathering space
Kamarka-marRoom / bedroom
Jinengji-nengRice barnTraditional storage for rice harvest
Koriko-riGate / entranceThe ceremonial split gate

Culture Note
The Compound as a Microcosm A traditional Balinese compound mirrors the cosmos. The family temple sits in the kaja-kangin (most sacred) corner. The kitchen and sleeping areas are in the middle. The animal pens and waste areas are in the kelod-kauh (least sacred) corner. Even within a single home, the mountain-sea axis organises everything. When you visit a Balinese family, noticing the sanggah and its position shows a level of cultural awareness that most visitors never have.

Aksara Bali — The Script

Overview
Aksara Bali is the traditional Balinese script, derived from the ancient Brahmi script of India. It is an abugida — a syllabic alphabet where each character represents a consonant with an inherent vowel sound that can be modified with diacritics. You will see it on temple gates, ceremonial banners, offerings, and road signs. You are not expected to read it fluently at this stage — but recognising the script and understanding its significance is part of genuine Balinese literacy.

Culture Note
A Living Script Aksara Bali is not a dead script preserved in museums — it is actively used in religious manuscripts (lontar), temple inscriptions, ceremonial banners (umbul-umbul), and road signs. Learning to recognise even a handful of characters will let you notice the script everywhere. Reading it fully is a Phase 4 goal, but even pointing to a sign and saying "That says 'Om Swastiastu'" will make any Balinese person smile.

Lesson 20

Phase 2 Review

Overview
You have now covered 20 lessons across Phases 1 and 2. This review consolidates what you know into a set of self-test prompts and connects the vocabulary into real-world scenarios. Before moving to Phase 3, you should be able to complete each of the tasks below comfortably.
Self-Test Checklist
  • Greet someone, introduce yourself, ask how they are, and close the conversation correctly — all in Balinese.
  • Say the same sentence in all three registers: I want to eat rice in Alus, Madia, and Kasar.
  • Count from 1 to 20 without looking. Say the price of five items in Rupiah.
  • Describe five objects around you using the noun + adjective rule.
  • Ask and answer all seven question words in a sentence each: dije, napi, sira, kenken, pidan, nguda, kuda.
  • Use ten verbs from Lesson 11 in a sentence. Negate five of them. Put three in the past using a time word.
  • Give someone directions using kaja, kelod, kangin, kauh, kiri, and tengen.
  • Express five emotions in a sentence: Tiang + emotion.
  • Name all four directional colours and their associated sacred directions.
  • Recognise and read aloud Om Swastiastu and Bali in Aksara Bali.
Phase 3 Preview

Phase 3: Conversation (Lessons 21–36)

Phase 3 moves from building blocks into flowing conversation. You will learn: how to tell stories and recount events using past/future framing, polite requests and formal address, ceremonial and temple vocabulary, deeper register navigation, compound sentences using connectors, expressing opinions and preferences, and the art of ngobrol — casual Balinese small talk.

Before starting Phase 3, spend at least one week using Phase 2 vocabulary in real conversations. The market, your warung, your neighbourhood — every interaction is a practice session. Balinese people are patient and generous with learners. Use that.
