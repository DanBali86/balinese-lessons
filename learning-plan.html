Lesson 01

Greetings & Politeness

Overview
Balinese greetings are deeply tied to Hinduism and community. The universal greeting Om Swastiastu is used by all Balinese regardless of caste or context. Unlike Indonesian, Balinese greetings shift depending on the social rank of the person you're addressing and the setting. Begin here — this is how every interaction opens.

Hello / Greetings (any time)
Om Swas-ti-as-tu
Hands pressed together, slight bow. Learn this first.
Tiang madan …
My name is …
Ti-ang ma-dan …
"Tiang" is the humble/polite first-person pronoun
Sira parab ragane?

Use with elders or strangers
Tiang nenten uning
I don't know (polite)

Culture Note
The Anjali Gesture Om Swastiastu is always paired with the anjali — palms pressed together at chest height, a slight forward bow. Greeting someone verbally without the gesture can feel incomplete. When entering a temple or meeting a high-caste elder, the bow is deeper. The body language carries as much meaning as the words.
Practice
  • Say the full greeting sequence: Om Swastiastu → introduce yourself → ask how someone is → respond Becik-becik → close with Om Santih Santih Santih Om.
  • Write out "My name is [your name]" and "Thank you very much" five times to internalize the spelling.
  • Notice the difference between formal Punapi gatra? and casual Kenken kabare? — which would you use with a street vendor? With a temple priest?

Lesson 02

Speech Registers: Sor Singgih Basa

Overview
This is the single most important structural feature of Balinese. The register system called Sor Singgih Basa has three main levels: Basa Alus (refined/high), Basa Madia (middle/polite), and Basa Kasar (low/everyday). The same concept has an entirely different word depending on who you're talking to. Learn this early or you will confuse and offend people.
Register Comparison
English Basa Alus High Basa Madia Mid Basa Kasar Low
I / meTitiangTiangIcang / Kai
YouRagane / IdaNyaneNyen / Iba
EatNgajengangNgajengNaar
SleepSirepPulesTuru
Go / walkLungaMamargiLuas
ComeRauhTekaMai
WaterToyaYehYeh
Good / fineBecikMelahApang
Want / wishKayunDotDemen
Grammar Note

When to use which register

Basa Alus (High): Use with elders, priests, high-caste Balinese, or in temple contexts. If unsure, default to Alus — it is never offensive to be too polite.

Basa Madia (Middle): Use in everyday polite conversation with strangers or people of unknown status. This is your safest starting register as a learner.

Basa Kasar (Low): Use only with close friends or family. Never use with strangers or elders.

Titiang kayun ngajengang I would like to eat — said to a priest or elder (Alus) I would like to eat
Icang dot naar I-cang dot na-ar — said to a close friend (Kasar) I wanna eat
Practice
  • Memorize the three ways to say "I" — Titiang (Alus), Tiang (Madia), Icang (Kasar) — drill them until instinctive.
  • Write one sentence saying "I want to eat" in all three registers using the correct vocabulary for each level.
  • Practice Madia as your baseline. Everything in Phase 1 defaults to Madia unless noted otherwise.

Numbers & Counting

Overview
Balinese has its own number system with distinct roots — not borrowed from Indonesian. Numbers are essential immediately: shopping, time, quantity, age. Treat each number as a new word, not a translation.

  • Practice saying prices: Satus lima dasa (150), Siu (1000), Dua siu (2000).

Pronouns & People

Overview
Pronouns shift with register. Balinese also avoids direct pronouns more than English — names and titles are often used instead of "you" or "he/she." This lesson builds your full pronoun toolkit.

Practice
  • When approaching a male stranger, use Bli instead of "you." Practice: Bli, tiang dot takon… (Bli, I want to ask…)
  • Drill: Tiang + verb in Madia. Say five sentences with Tiang as subject.
  • Note that Ida means both "you" (Alus) AND "he/she" (Alus) — context determines meaning.

Family & Relationships

Overview
Family is central to Balinese social life. Balinese people often introduce themselves by their birth-order name. Understanding these terms will immediately open conversations and show cultural awareness that goes far beyond simple vocabulary.

Culture Note
Birth Order as Identity If someone introduces themselves as "Wayan," you immediately know they are the first-born child. This applies across all castes. Asking "Wayan, Made, Nyoman, atawa Ketut?" is a natural and friendly opener that signals you understand the culture. Balinese people are genuinely surprised and delighted when visitors know this system.

Lesson 07

Time & Days

Overview
Balinese has two parallel calendar systems — the Gregorian calendar for daily life and the traditional Pawukon calendar (210-day cycle) for ceremonies. Focus on Gregorian-based day vocabulary and time expressions first. Pawukon day names are a Phase 3 goal.

Culture Note
Sanskrit Day Names Balinese day names are Sanskrit-derived, like the planets: Soma (Moon/Monday), Anggara (Mars/Tuesday), Budha (Mercury/Wednesday). This connection to Sanskrit helps with memorization and reflects the deep Hindu roots of the language. Also note: Balinese does not have time-of-day greetings like "good morning" — Om Swastiastu works at all hours.

Lesson 08

Body & Health

Overview
In Balinese Hindu tradition, different parts of the body carry spiritual significance — the head is sacred, the feet are the lowest. This shapes everyday behavior, from how you pass objects to how you position yourself in a temple. Learn the words alongside their cultural weight.
Body Vocabulary
BalineseRomanizationEnglishNote
AwakA-wakBodyAlso means "self"
SirahSi-rahHeadSacred — never touch another's head
MataMa-taEye(s)
CunguhCu-nguhNose
BibihBi-bihMouth / lips
LimaLi-maHand / armSame word as "five"
BatisBa-tisLeg / footNever point feet at shrines or people
PanasPa-nasHot / fever
SakitSa-kitSick / painShared with Indonesian
Tiang gelemTi-ang ge-lemI am sickSpecifically Balinese term
BalianBa-li-anTraditional healerNot the same as a doctor
Culture Note
The Sacred Head / Profane Feet The Balinese body is organized on a vertical spiritual axis. The head (sirah) is closest to the divine — never touch someone's head without permission. The feet (batis) are the most impure — never point them at a shrine, sacred object, or another person. When sitting in a temple, tuck your feet behind or to the side. These are expressions of respect that every Balinese person will notice.

Phase 2 Preview: Building Blocks (Lessons 9–20)

Phase 2 builds on these foundations with: basic sentence structure and verb patterns, adjectives and descriptors, directions and navigation, emotions and states, common question words (kenken, napi, sira, dija, nguda), verbs conjugated in Madia register, and an introduction to Aksara Bali (the Balinese script) for reading awareness.

Review Phase 1 vocabulary daily — 10 minutes of active recall per lesson is more effective than long sessions. Say Om Swastiastu to every Balinese person you encounter. This alone will open more doors than any textbook.
