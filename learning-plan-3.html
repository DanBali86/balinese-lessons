Connectors & Conjunctions

Overview
Until now most of your sentences have been single-clause. Connectors are what turn those individual sentences into flowing speech. Indonesian conjunctions are mostly short, simple words — and once you have them, your sentences immediately sound more natural and complex. This is the gateway to real conversation.

Practice
  • Take any two sentences you already know and join them with dan, tapi, karena, or jadi. Start simple and build up.
  • Practise the if-then structure: Kalau … saya … Make five different if-then sentences about your daily life.
  • Use sebelum and setelah to describe your morning routine: Sebelum makan, saya minum kopi.
  • Jadi is the word you will hear most in natural Indonesian speech — it fills the role of "so," "well," "then," and "therefore." Start noticing it everywhere.

Telling Stories

Overview
Storytelling in Indonesian uses time markers, sequence words, and aspect markers to build a narrative. Since verbs don't conjugate, the story is held together entirely by these words. Once you master the sequence, you can recount anything that happened — a trip, a meal, a conversation, an incident — in natural, flowing Indonesian.

Kemarin malamke-ma-rin · ma-lamLast night

Sample Story
Story
Kemarin saya pergi ke pasar. Pertama-tama saya beli buah, lalu saya makan nasi campur di warung dekat sana. Tiba-tiba hujan deras. Akhirnya saya pulang naik ojek. Ternyata ojeknya orang Bali asli — jadi kami ngobrol pakai bahasa Indonesia.
Yesterday I went to the market. First I bought fruit, then I ate nasi campur at a nearby warung. Suddenly it rained heavily. In the end I went home by ojek. It turned out the driver was a local Balinese — so we chatted in Indonesian.
Insight
Ternyata
Ternyata is one of the most characteristically Indonesian words in storytelling. It introduces a revelation or surprise — "it turned out that…" or "apparently…". Native speakers use it constantly when recounting events. If you use ternyata naturally in a story, Indonesians will immediately feel you understand how their language flows.
Practice
  • Recount yesterday in Indonesian using at least five narrative words: kemarin, pertama, lalu, akhirnya, ternyata.
  • Tell a story about something that went wrong — use tiba-tiba for the turning point.
  • Practise ternyata — find three things from your week that could be introduced with "it turned out that…" and say them in Indonesian.

Opinions & Preferences

Overview
Expressing opinions is where language becomes genuinely personal. Indonesian has a range of opinion phrases from very direct to very hedged — and choosing between them is as much cultural as linguistic. Indonesians tend to express disagreement indirectly to preserve face, while enthusiasm and praise are expressed freely and openly.
Opinion Phrases

Saya rasasa-ya · ra-saI feel / I thinkSlightly softer than menurut saya
Saya pikirsa-ya · pi-kirI think (cognitively)More rational/analytical tone
Saya setujusa-ya · se-tu-juI agree

Kurangku-rangNot quite / lacking / less than idealThe polite way to say something is not good
Lumayanlu-ma-yanPretty good / decent / not badThe Indonesian "bof" — manages expectations
Comparing & Contrasting

Lebih … daripada / dari

To compare two things, use lebih (more) + adjective + daripada / dari (than):

Kopi ini lebih enak daripada yang kemarin ko-pi · i-ni · le-bih · e-nak · da-ri-pa-da · yang · ke-ma-rin This coffee is more delicious than yesterday's
Saya lebih suka nasi goreng dari mie goreng sa-ya · le-bih · su-ka · na-si · go-reng · da-ri · mi-e · go-reng I prefer fried rice to fried noodles

For superlatives, use paling (most) before the adjective:

Ini yang paling enak yang pernah saya makan i-ni · yang · pa-ling · e-nak · yang · per-nah · sa-ya · ma-kan This is the most delicious thing I've ever eaten
Culture Note
Kurang & Lumayan — The Art of Indirect Feedback Indonesians rarely say something is bad directly. Instead, kurang (lacking / not quite) does the work — "kurang enak" means "not that tasty" without being harsh. Lumayan (decent / pretty good) manages expectations politely. If someone asks how your Indonesian is and you say "lumayan," they know you're being modest. If they say your cooking is "lumayan," they might mean it's just okay. Reading the difference is part of cultural fluency.

Indonesian Particles

Overview
Indonesian has a set of small particles — lah, kah, sih, dong, deh, kok, ya — that have no direct English equivalent but appear constantly in spoken Indonesian. They add tone, attitude, emphasis, and social meaning to sentences. Without them, Indonesian sounds technically correct but flat. With them, you sound like a real speaker. This is one of the most distinctive features of the language.
The Particles
Lah
Softens a statement or command. Makes it sound less abrupt, more reassuring.
Sudahlah → Just let it go / It's fine Pergi lah → Just go (friendly push)
Kah
Turns a statement into a formal question. Rarely used in casual speech.
Apakah kamu makan? → Did you eat? (formal) Benarkah? → Is that true? (formal)
Sih
Adds mild exasperation, curiosity, or emphasis. Often signals slight frustration or pressing for an answer.
Kenapa sih? → Why though? / What's the deal? Apa sih itu? → What even is that?
Dong
Makes a request or suggestion more persuasive or friendly. Like "come on" in English.
Bantu dong → Help me, come on Ikut dong → Come along, will you?
Deh
Signals reluctant agreement or a softened concession. "Fine," "alright then," or "go ahead."
Iya deh → Alright fine Terserah deh → Whatever, up to you
Kok
Expresses mild surprise or contradiction. "But…" or "How come…"
Kok bisa? → How is that possible? Kok mahal? → How come it's expensive?
Ya
The most versatile particle — seeks agreement, softens requests, confirms understanding.
Iya ya → Yeah right? / I know right Tolong ya → Please, okay?
Nih / Tuh
Nih = here (pointing at something). Tuh = there / see? (drawing attention).
Nih → Here you go / here it is Tuh kan? → See? I told you so
Particles in Natural Dialogue
A
Kok belum makan sih?
How come you haven't eaten yet?
B
Lagi sibuk nih. Makan nanti deh.
I'm busy right now. I'll eat later, fine.
A
Makan dong, penting tuh!
Come on, eat — it's important!
B
Iya iya, sudahlah.
Okay okay, alright already.
Practice
  • Start by noticing particles in Indonesian conversation, TV, or YouTube. They will be everywhere once you know what to listen for.
  • Practise dong — make five requests using it. It immediately makes you sound more like a natural speaker.
  • Use kok to express surprise at something today: Kok mahal banget? (How come it's so expensive?)
  • End a few sentences with ya as a soft confirmation-seeker. Notice how it changes the tone compared to saying nothing.

The Ber- Prefix

Overview
The ber- prefix is the second major verb prefix after me-. It creates intransitive verbs — verbs that describe a state, activity, or action the subject does without a direct object. You met bertemu (to meet) in Lesson 14 — that's a ber- verb. Many essential everyday verbs use this prefix.
How Ber- Works

Base word → Ber- verb

Ber- attaches to the front of a base word. If the base starts with "r", the prefix becomes be- only (no doubling). The resulting verb is always intransitive — no direct object follows it.

bicara → berbicara bi-ca-ra → ber-bi-ca-ra talk → to speak / to have a conversation
kerja → bekerja ker-ja → be-ker-ja work → to work
renang → berenang re-nang → be-re-nang swim → to swim

Practice
  • Note that belajar is already a ber- verb — you have been using it from the start. Recognise which other verbs you already know are ber- verbs.
  • Build sentences: Saya bekerja di Bali. Saya belajar bahasa Indonesia setiap hari. Kita bisa bertemu besok.
  • Compare: Saya berbicara bahasa Indonesia (formal) vs Saya ngomong bahasa Indonesia (casual). Both are correct in the right context.

The Ter- Prefix

Overview
The ter- prefix has two distinct uses — and both come up constantly. First, it creates superlatives (the most, the biggest, the best). Second, it creates accidental or involuntary verbs (to accidentally do something, or something that happened without intention). Both are essential for natural speech.
Ter- for Superlatives

Ter- + adjective = the most ___

terbesar ter-be-sar the biggest (besar = big)
terenak ter-e-nak the most delicious
terdekat ter-de-kat the nearest (you already used this in directions!)
terbaik ter-ba-ik the best
Ter- for Accidental Actions

Ter- + verb = accidentally / unintentionally

Saya terjatuh sa-ya · ter-ja-tuh I fell (accidentally) — jatuh = fall
Pintu terbuka pin-tu · ter-bu-ka The door opened (by itself / accidentally)
Saya tertidur di sofa sa-ya · ter-ti-dur · di · so-fa I fell asleep on the sofa (unintentionally)
Kopi itu terminum ko-pi · i-tu · ter-mi-num The coffee got drunk (by accident / somehow)
Practice
  • Use ter- superlatives to describe things in your life: Warung terenak di sini, kopi terbaik yang pernah saya minum.
  • Practise accidental ter- verbs by recounting something that happened without intention this week.
  • Notice that ternyata (from Lesson 22) is also a ter- word — from nyata (real/clear). It "became apparent" — an involuntary realisation. The prefix is everywhere.

Passive Voice with Di-

Overview
Indonesian uses the passive voice far more frequently than English. The di- prefix creates passive verbs — the object of an action becomes the subject. This is not just a formal construction: Indonesians use it constantly in everyday speech, sometimes more often than the active voice. Understanding it is essential for comprehension.
Active vs Passive

Me- (active) → Di- (passive)

The pattern is simple: where me- makes an active verb, di- makes the passive equivalent. The object of the active sentence becomes the subject of the passive sentence.

Saya membeli nasi goreng (active) sa-ya · mem-be-li · na-si · go-reng I bought the fried rice
Nasi goreng dibeli saya (passive) na-si · go-reng · di-be-li · sa-ya The fried rice was bought by me
Rumah itu dibangun tahun 1990 ru-mah · i-tu · di-ba-ngun · ta-hun · 1990 That house was built in 1990
Bahasa Indonesia dipakai di seluruh Indonesia ba-ha-sa · in-do-ne-si-a · di-pa-kai · di · se-lu-ruh · in-do-ne-si-a Indonesian is used throughout Indonesia
Common Di- Verbs
Di- VerbPronunciationEnglish
Dimakandi-ma-kanIs eaten / was eaten
Diminumdi-mi-numIs drunk / was drunk
Dibuatdi-bu-atIs made / was made
Dijualdi-ju-alIs sold / for sale

Insight
Dilarang
You will see Dilarang … on signs everywhere in Indonesia and Bali — Dilarang merokok (No smoking), Dilarang masuk (No entry), Dilarang parkir (No parking). It literally means "is forbidden to…" — a passive construction used for rules and prohibitions. Recognising it is immediately practical.

Shopping & Bargaining

Overview
You covered basic market vocabulary in Phase 2. This lesson takes you to full bargaining fluency — the complete negotiation arc from browsing to purchase, including how to politely push back, make counteroffers, and close a deal. Bargaining is a social ritual in Indonesian markets, not just a transaction.
Full Bargaining Dialogue
You
Permisi, ini berapa harganya?
Excuse me, how much is this?
Seller
Seratus lima puluh ribu.
150,000 Rupiah.
You
Wah, mahal sekali. Bisa kurang tidak?
Wow, that's very expensive. Can you lower it?
Seller
Ini harga bagus sudah. Seratus dua puluh ribu, final.
This is already a good price. 120,000, final.
You
Bagaimana kalau sembilan puluh ribu? Saya beli dua sekalian.
How about 90,000? I'll buy two at the same time.
Seller
Oke deh, seratus ribu untuk dua ya.
Okay fine, 100,000 for two then.
You
Deal. Ini uangnya. Ada kembalian tidak?
Deal. Here's the money. Do you have change?

Bagaimana kalau … ?

ba-gai-ma-na · ka-lau …?

Indonesian Small Talk

Overview
Ngobrol — casual Indonesian chitchat — is the conversational glue of daily life. Indonesians are warm, socially engaged, and love talking. Mastering a handful of small-talk topics will open far more doors than any amount of grammar study. These are the conversations that happen at warungs, in ojeks, at the market, and with neighbours every single day.
Classic Small-Talk Topics & Phrases
Sudah lama di Bali?
Have you been in Bali long?
su-dah · la-ma · di · ba-li?
The most common opener for foreigners
Suka Bali?
Do you like Bali?
su-ka · ba-li?
Answer: Suka banget! / Sangat suka!
Kerja apa di sini?
What do you do here?
ker-ja · a-pa · di · si-ni?
Very natural, not intrusive — just friendly curiosity
Sudah coba nasi jinggo?
Have you tried nasi jinggo yet?
su-dah · co-ba · na-si · jing-go?
Food recommendations are a primary social currency
Bahasa Indonesianya bagus!
Your Indonesian is good!
ba-ha-sa · in-do-ne-si-a-nya · ba-gus!
You will hear this — the correct response is "Masih belajar" (still learning)
Masih belajar, tapi suka sekali
Still learning, but I really love it
ma-sih · be-la-jar · ta-pi · su-ka · se-ka-li
The perfect humble response to compliments on your Indonesian
Culture Note
Ngobrol — The Value of Casual Connection In Indonesian culture, taking a few minutes to chat — even briefly — is not wasted time. It is relationship-building. A foreigner who attempts Indonesian small talk, even imperfectly, is showing respect and genuine interest. Indonesians will almost always respond with warmth, patience, and encouragement. The phrase masih belajar (still learning) is your best friend — say it with a smile and people will immediately become your teachers.

Indonesian Media & News

Overview
Engaging with Indonesian media — news, social media, YouTube, podcasts — is one of the fastest ways to accelerate from conversational to fluent. Indonesian media uses formal Bahasa Indonesia, which is worth distinguishing from the casual spoken language you use daily. This lesson bridges that gap and gives you the vocabulary to engage with Indonesian content independently.

Recommended Indonesian Media

How to use media for language learning

YouTube: Search for Indonesian content with subtitles. Start with channels that have Indonesian captions — you can read along as you listen. Vlogs, cooking channels, and travel content use natural conversational Indonesian.

Podcasts: "Podcast Subjektif," "On The Way," and Indonesian news podcasts use clear, standard Indonesian — good for training your ear to formal speech.

Instagram / TikTok: Follow Indonesian accounts in topics you already care about. Captions and comments are written in natural, casual Indonesian — excellent for absorbing particles, slang, and how sentences actually look in writing.

WhatsApp: If you have Indonesian contacts, reading and responding to messages is one of the most effective immersion tools available. Even emoji reactions require understanding context.

Lesson 36

Phase 3 Review

Overview
You have now completed 36 lessons across three phases. At this point you have the vocabulary, grammar, and conversational tools to handle most everyday situations in Indonesia confidently. This review consolidates Phase 3 and sets the direction for Phase 4 — fluency.
Self-Test Checklist
  • Tell a story about something that happened last week using at least five narrative words: tadi, kemarin, lalu, tiba-tiba, akhirnya, ternyata.
  • Join ten pairs of sentences using different connectors: dan, tapi, karena, jadi, kalau, walaupun, sebelum, setelah, ketika, lalu.
  • Express an opinion on three topics using menurut saya, saya rasa, lebih suka, and paling suka. Include a comparison using lebih … daripada.
  • Use five particles naturally in sentences: dong, sih, kok, deh, ya. Explain what each one adds to the tone.
  • Give five examples of ber- verbs in sentences. Give five examples of ter- verbs — two as superlatives, three as accidental actions.
  • Convert three active me- sentences into passive di- sentences.
  • Complete a full restaurant scenario in Indonesian from arrival to paying the bill.
  • Make, accept, and decline a plan in Indonesian, using yuk, janjian, tidak bisa, and lain kali.
  • Describe your daily work routine using setiap hari, sebelum, setelah, and berangkat / pulang kerja.
  • Say five emergency phrases from memory, including Tolong, darurat, and how to describe a symptom.
Phase 4 Preview

Phase 4: Fluency (Lessons 37+)

Phase 4 is about closing the gap between competent and genuinely fluent. You will work on: advanced prefix and suffix combinations (me-…-kan, me-…-i, ke-…-an), nuanced register switching, Indonesian idioms and proverbs (peribahasa), formal writing and email Indonesian, understanding rapid natural speech, regional vocabulary differences between Java and Bali, and developing your own personal Indonesian voice.

By the time you reach Phase 4, the most important thing is immersion. Seek out Indonesian conversations every day. Make Indonesian friends. Read Indonesian content. Watch Indonesian media without subtitles. Language fluency is not achieved in a classroom — it is built in the world, through thousands of small interactions, mistakes, and moments of real connection.

Selamat belajar — dan terus semangat. Happy studying — and keep going.
