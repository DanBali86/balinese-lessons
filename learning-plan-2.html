Lesson 09

Adjectives & Descriptors

Overview
Indonesian adjectives follow the same rule as Balinese — they come after the noun, not before it. So "a big house" is rumah besar (house big). The intensifier sekali (very) always follows the adjective: besar sekali (very big). This word order is consistent and applies to all adjectives without exception.
Word Order Rule

Noun + Adjective (not Adjective + Noun)

English: big house  →  Indonesian: rumah besar

English: cold water  →  Indonesian: air dingin

English: delicious food  →  Indonesian: makanan enak

Rumah itu besar sekali ru-mah · i-tu · be-sar · se-ka-li That house is very big
Kopi ini enak banget! ko-pi · i-ni · e-nak · ba-nget! This coffee is really delicious! (banget = very, informal)

Practice
  • Describe five things around you using noun + adjective order: kopi panas, rumah besar, jalan panjang.
  • Add intensifiers: panas sekali, enak banget, mahal sekali. Note that sekali is formal and banget is casual — both are correct.
  • Build full sentences: Kopi ini panas sekali (This coffee is very hot). Rumah itu kecil tapi bagus (That house is small but nice).
  • Adjectives never change form for gender or number — besar works for one or many, male or female.

Question Words

Overview
Indonesian question words are mostly placed at the beginning or end of a sentence — unlike English where they always go at the front. You can often make a question simply by adding a question word to a normal statement. Mastering these unlocks real two-way conversation.

Di mana vs Ke mana vs Dari mana

The three "where" questions

This distinction is one of the most useful and logical features of Indonesian:

Di mana kamu tinggal? di · ma-na · ka-mu · ting-gal? Where do you live? (location — di = at)
Ke mana kamu pergi? ke · ma-na · ka-mu · per-gi? Where are you going? (direction — ke = to)
Dari mana asalmu? da-ri · ma-na · a-sal-mu? Where are you from? (origin — dari = from)
Question Sentence Examples
Apa yang kamu makan?
What are you eating?
Yang = that / which (linker)
Siapa nama Anda?
What is your name? (lit. Who is your name?)
Kapan kamu datang?
When are you coming?
Kenapa kamu tidak makan?
Why aren't you eating?
Gimana kabarnya?
How are things? / How is it going? (casual)
Practice
  • Drill the three "where" questions until the di/ke/dari distinction is instinctive. They come up constantly.
  • Build a question with every question word using a verb you already know.
  • Practice casual vs formal pairs: bagaimana / gimana, mengapa / kenapa. You will hear the casual versions far more often in speech.

Essential Verbs

Overview
You already know makan, minum, masak, and pergi. This lesson adds 20 high-frequency verbs that cover most daily activities. Remember: Indonesian verbs never conjugate for person, tense, or number. The same verb form works for everyone at all times — tense comes from time words and markers like sudah, sedang, and akan.

Insight
Tahu vs Kenal
Like French (savoir/connaître) or Spanish (saber/conocer), Indonesian distinguishes between knowing a fact (tahu) and knowing a person (kenal). Saya tidak tahu = I don't know (the answer / the fact). Saya tidak kenal dia = I don't know them (personally). Using the wrong one is a common beginner mistake — notice it now.
Practice
  • Build five sentences: one with mau, one with bisa, one with harus, one with sudah, one with tidak.
  • Practice tahu vs kenal — say "I know" in five contexts and decide which word is correct each time.
  • Combine question words with new verbs: Ke mana kamu pergi? Kapan kamu datang? Kenapa kamu tidak tidur?

The Me- Verb Prefix

Overview
Indonesian has a rich prefix system that transforms base words into verbs. The most important is me-, which creates active transitive verbs — verbs where a subject does something to an object. You will hear base verbs in casual speech, but me- verbs appear constantly in formal Indonesian, news, and writing. Understanding this prefix unlocks a huge part of Indonesian vocabulary automatically.
How Me- Works

Base verb → Me- verb

The me- prefix changes form depending on the first letter of the base word — but the logic is consistent once you know it.

makan → memakan ma-kan → me-ma-kan eat → to eat (formal/active)
beli → membeli be-li → mem-be-li buy → to buy
lihat → melihat li-hat → me-li-hat see → to see
tulis → menulis tu-lis → me-nu-lis write → to write

In everyday casual speech, the me- prefix is often dropped. But in formal contexts — talking to authorities, reading, writing — always use the full form.

Culture Note
Formal vs Casual Verb Use In casual spoken Indonesian you will hear "dia beli nasi" (she buy rice) — the base verb with no prefix. In formal Indonesian, on the news, or in writing, you would say "dia membeli nasi." As a learner, both forms are correct in the right context. Start with base verbs in conversation and add the me- prefix when you want to sound more formal or are writing. Indonesians will understand you perfectly either way.

Directions & Navigation

Overview
Indonesian directions are straightforward and use the standard compass alongside practical left/right/straight vocabulary. Unlike Balinese which uses the sacred mountain-sea axis, Indonesian uses conventional directions that are immediately transferable from English — making this one of the easiest lessons in the plan.

Emotions & States

Overview
Expressing how you feel in Indonesian opens up genuine human connection. The structure is simple: Saya + [feeling/state]. Indonesian is expressive and emotional vocabulary is used freely in everyday conversation — Indonesians readily share how they feel and expect you to do the same.

Culture Note
Malu — More Than Embarrassment The concept of malu (shame/embarrassment/shyness) is central to Indonesian social culture. Causing someone to feel malu — to lose face in front of others — is one of the most serious social offences. Indonesians will go to great lengths to avoid publicly embarrassing themselves or others. This explains why direct confrontation, criticism, and saying "no" bluntly are often avoided in favour of indirect, face-saving communication. Understanding malu will help you navigate Indonesian social dynamics far more than any vocabulary list.

Colours & Descriptions

Overview

At the Market

Overview
The pasar (market) is the best place to practice real Indonesian from day one. This lesson combines everything from Phases 1 and 2 — numbers, adjectives, verbs, and question words — into practical market conversation. Bargaining is expected and normal at traditional markets; fixed price shops (supermarkets) are different.

  • Learn to understand the numbers in the response — vendors will tell you a price and you need to recognise it.

Weather & Nature

Overview
Indonesia straddles the equator and has two seasons: the dry season (musim kemarau) and the wet/rainy season (musim hujan). Weather is one of the most natural conversation topics and the vocabulary is immediately usable. Bali's tropical climate means panas (hot) and hujan (rain) will come up every day.

Phase 2 Review

Overview
You have now covered 20 lessons across Phases 1 and 2 of Bahasa Indonesia. This review consolidates what you know into a self-test and connects the vocabulary into real-world scenarios. Before moving to Phase 3, you should be able to complete each task below comfortably.
Self-Test Checklist
  • Greet someone correctly for the time of day, introduce yourself, ask how they are, and close the conversation — all in Indonesian.
  • Say the same sentence using tidak and bukan correctly. Explain the difference.
  • Count from 1 to 100 in multiples of 10. Say three prices in Rupiah above 100,000.
  • Use saya, kita, and kami in a sentence each — and explain the kita/kami distinction.
  • Describe five objects using noun + adjective order with an intensifier.
  • Use all ten question words in a sentence each: apa, siapa, di mana, ke mana, dari mana, kapan, kenapa, bagaimana, berapa, yang mana.
  • Use sudah, sedang, and akan with the same verb to express past, present, and future.
  • Give someone directions using kiri, kanan, lurus, belok, dekat, and jauh.
  • Express five emotions: Saya + emotion.
  • Name all eleven colours. Describe a light and dark shade using muda and tua.
  • Complete a market transaction in Indonesian from start to finish — ask price, bargain, agree, pay, get change.
  • Explain the me- verb prefix and give five examples of base verb → me- verb.
Phase 3 Preview

Phase 3: Conversation (Lessons 21–36)

Phase 3 moves from building blocks into flowing conversation. You will learn: how to tell stories and recount events, polite requests and formal address, the ber- and ter- verb prefixes, compound sentences using connectors (dan, tapi, karena, kalau), expressing opinions and preferences, Indonesian particles (lah, kah, sih, dong, deh), the passive voice using di-, and the art of Indonesian small talk.

Before starting Phase 3, spend at least one week using Phase 1 and 2 vocabulary in real daily conversations. Order food, ask for directions, chat about the weather, negotiate at the market. Every interaction is a practice session — and Indonesians are among the most patient and welcoming people in the world when someone tries to speak their language.
