Extended Sentence Formula

S   Subject
+
V   Verb
+
O   Object
+
Adv   Adverb / Time
New this lesson: Your tutor extended the S+V+O structure by adding an Adverb slot. This covers time words (kemarin, besok, sekarang), place words (di rumah, di restoran), and manner words. The adverb can go at the end of the sentence or the very beginning for emphasis — either is correct.
Future tense: Add akan between the subject and verb to express "will." The verb itself never changes — only akan signals futurity. Saya masak = I cook. Saya akan masak = I will cook.
Negation reminder: Tidak sits between subject and verb — or between subject and akan for future negation. Saya tidak akan masak = I will not cook.

04

Connectors

Dan, Tapi, Atau — joining sentences and ideas

Your tutor circled these three in red — they are the core connectors for building more complex sentences. All three sit between the two clauses or items they connect.

Saya makan nasi dan minum teh sa-ya · ma-kan · na-si · dan · mi-num · teh I eat rice and drink tea
Saya lapar tapi tidak mau makan sa-ya · la-par · ta-pi · ti-dak · mau · ma-kan I'm hungry but don't want to eat
Kamu mau makan di sini atau di restoran? ka-mu · mau · ma-kan · di · si-ni · a-tau · di · res-to-ran? Do you want to eat here or at the restaurant?
05

Question Types

Two types of question

Your tutor drew the split clearly: questions are either Yes/No (Y/N) or Wh- questions.

Yes/No questions — no extra word needed. Take a normal statement and raise your intonation at the end. The sentence structure stays identical.

Saya masak pizza? → Kamu masak pizza? ka-mu · ma-sak · piz-za? Are you cooking pizza? (rising intonation only)

Wh- questions — add the question word, usually at the front of the sentence. The rest of the sentence stays in normal S-V-O order.

06

Meals & Time of Day

Meals use makan + time of day

Your tutor showed that Indonesian doesn't have separate words for "have breakfast / lunch / dinner" as actions — instead it's simply makan (eat) combined with the time-of-day word. The meals are named after when they happen.

Sarapan = makan pagi  →  Breakfast (eat in the morning) sa-ra-pan / ma-kan · pa-gi Both forms are used — sarapan is more common as a noun
Makan siang  →  Lunch (eat at midday) ma-kan · si-ang Siang = the midday period
Makan malam  →  Dinner (eat in the evening) ma-kan · ma-lam Malam = night/evening

Why this matters
These time periods directly determine which greeting to use. Now that you know the clock ranges, you can always choose the right Selamat … greeting. Subuh is the one your tutor added that doesn't appear in a greeting — it's simply the very early pre-dawn period (relevant for anyone catching early flights or sunrise surfs).

08

Practice Drills

Drill these until automatic
  • Build the full future sentence from your notes out loud: Saya akan masak pizza besok. Then negate it: Saya tidak akan masak pizza besok. Then question it: Kamu akan masak pizza besok?
  • Swap time words through a sentence: Saya makan sekarang / kemarin / besok / hari ini. Notice the verb never changes — only the time word moves.
  • Practise all three connectors in one sentence chain: Saya mau makan nasi dan minum teh, tapi restoran tutup, jadi saya masak di dapur.
  • Drill all six question words by asking yourself a question with each one about your day: Di mana saya makan tadi? Kapan saya akan minum kopi? Siapa yang masak?
  • Name the correct Indonesian time period for right now — and which greeting goes with it. Do this every time you check your phone today.
  • Practice the meal formula: say breakfast, lunch, and dinner in Indonesian three ways — as makan + time and as the single noun form (sarapan / makan siang / makan malam).
  • Build one sentence using di as a location marker: Saya akan makan di restoran malam ini. Then swap the location: di rumah, di dapur, di sini.
