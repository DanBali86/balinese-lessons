Lesson 01

Greetings & Politeness

Overview
Indonesian greetings are time-sensitive — unlike English's "hello," Indonesians use different greetings depending on the time of day. The key phrase Selamat (safe / blessed) prefixes most greetings. Indonesian is one of the easiest languages to pronounce — it is almost entirely phonetic, spelled exactly as it sounds using the Latin alphabet.

Culture Note
Pronunciation — The Good News Indonesian is written in the Latin alphabet and is almost perfectly phonetic. Every letter is pronounced consistently, there are no tones (unlike Mandarin or Thai), and stress patterns are simple. The letter C is always pronounced "ch" (like in "chair"). The letter J is pronounced like the English "j" in "jump." Once you know the sounds, you can read any Indonesian word aloud correctly. This makes it one of the most accessible languages in the world for English speakers to start speaking immediately.
Practice
  • Practice all four time-based greetings out loud: Selamat pagi, selamat siang, selamat sore, selamat malam. Which would you use right now?
  • Run through the full introduction sequence from memory: greet someone → say your name → ask their name → say you're learning Indonesian → say goodbye.
  • Note the difference between selamat tinggal (you're leaving) and selamat jalan (they're leaving). Use the right one.
  • Say terima kasih to every person who helps you today. Notice how naturally it comes after a few uses.

Lesson 02

Formal vs Informal Indonesian

Overview
Unlike Balinese which has a complex multi-tier register system, Indonesian has two main registers: Bahasa Formal (used in writing, education, business, and with strangers) and Bahasa Informal/Gaul (used in everyday speech, with friends, and in casual settings). The same idea can have very different words. As a beginner, learn formal first — it is never offensive — and pick up informal naturally from conversation.

Tidak vs Bukan — The Key Negation Rule

Two different words for "not"

Tidak negates verbs and adjectives.

Bukan negates nouns and pronouns.

Saya tidak makan sa-ya · ti-dak · ma-kan I don't eat / I'm not eating — (tidak + verb)
Ini bukan nasi i-ni · bu-kan · na-si This is not rice — (bukan + noun)
Saya bukan orang Indonesia sa-ya · bu-kan · o-rang · in-do-ne-si-a I am not Indonesian — (bukan + noun/identity)

In casual speech, nggak often replaces both tidak and bukan. But in formal contexts and for clarity, use the correct one.

Culture Note
Bahasa Indonesia — A Unifying Language Indonesian is the national language of a country with over 700 regional languages. It was deliberately chosen and standardised in 1945 as a neutral lingua franca — not the language of any single ethnic group, which is why Javanese (the most widely spoken native language) was not chosen. This means Indonesians are generally very tolerant and encouraging of foreigners learning the language, because they understand it is a second language for most Indonesians too. Your mistakes will be met with patience, not judgement.
Practice
  • Write five sentences using tidak and five using bukan. Check each one — is it negating a verb/adjective or a noun?
  • Learn to recognise nggak and udah — you will hear these constantly in casual Indonesian speech.
  • Practice the same sentence in both registers: Saya tidak mau (formal) → Aku nggak mau (informal).

Numbers & Counting

Overview
Indonesian numbers are logical and highly regular. Once you know 1–10, the pattern for 11–19 and all tens is immediately clear. There are no irregular numbers. This makes Indonesian arithmetic vocabulary one of the easiest to master of any language.

Pronouns & People

Overview
Indonesian pronouns are straightforward but shift between formal and informal registers. One important feature: Indonesian uses titles as pronouns far more than English does — addressing someone as Bapak (Mr/Sir) or Ibu (Mrs/Ma'am) rather than "you" is the polite norm with strangers and elders.

Kita vs Kami

The inclusive/exclusive "we" distinction

This is one of the most interesting features of Indonesian — and one that English lacks entirely.

Kita = we, including you (the listener is part of the group)

Kami = we, not including you (the listener is outside the group)

Kita pergi makan, yuk! ki-ta · per-gi · ma-kan · yuk! Let's go eat! (you're invited — inclusive)
Kami sudah makan ka-mi · su-dah · ma-kan We have already eaten (you were not with us — exclusive)
Practice
  • Address the next older man you interact with as Pak instead of "you." Notice how natural it feels in context.
  • Use kita and kami correctly in two sentences each. Think carefully about whether the listener is included.
  • Practice both registers: Saya mau pergi (formal) → Aku mau pergi (informal). Same meaning, different feel.

Family & Relationships

Overview
Family is central to Indonesian culture and one of the most common conversation topics with strangers. Indonesians will often ask about your family status, children, and origins within the first few minutes of meeting you — not as intrusion, but as warm social connection. Time & Days

Overview
Indonesian handles tense entirely through time words — the verb never changes. This is one of the features that makes Indonesian grammar so learnable. Saya makan means I eat / I ate / I will eat — you add time words to specify. Sudah (already) and akan (will) are the key tense markers.

Tense in Practice

No verb conjugation — ever

The verb makan (eat) never changes form. Tense is expressed entirely by time words and markers:

Saya sedang makan sa-ya · se-dang · ma-kan I am eating right now (sedang = currently)
Saya sudah makan sa-ya · su-dah · ma-kan I have already eaten (sudah = already)
Saya akan makan besok sa-ya · a-kan · ma-kan · be-sok I will eat tomorrow (akan = will + besok = tomorrow)
Practice
  • Say all seven days of the week out loud. Note that Minggu means both Sunday and "week."
  • Take any verb you know and say it in three tenses using sedang, sudah, and akan.
  • Practice asking and telling the time: Jam berapa sekarang? Sekarang jam…
